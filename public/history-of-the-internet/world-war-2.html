Sputnik, USSR, and the Birth of the Internet

The Soviets have seemed to outwin even the most brilliant inventors and innovators during the 50’s. As the giddy Americans focused on how to better their cars or improve television sets, the Soviets were scheming on something that was more sensible – concocting Sputnik, the world’s first manmade satellite. October 4, 1957, thus the birth of triumph over Cold War.

And just as how some of us have grandfathers who fought during the war, ARPAnet, grandfather to the Internet, contributed as much too. Communication between the military was very confidential that a protocol called Network Control Protocol was developed. They exchanged messages via the NCP, in which ARPAnet guarded the flow of information.

This generation is lucky enough to send as much data as they want to because way back in 1969, Internet’s grandfather sent its first one word message via “node-to-node” communicaton but the system crashed. The word was “LOGIN” and unfortunately only “LO” was received.

On 1969, only four computers were connected to ARPAnet, one was installed in a research lab in UCLA, and one located in Stanford. On 1970’s, three more computers added: University of Hawaii’s ALOHAnet followed by London’s University College and Norway’s the Royal Radar Establishment. The networks grew steady but it was difficult to put them together as one.

Vinton Cerf, a computer scientist, later found a solution to this dilemma as he discovered “Transmission Control Protocol” in which one writer described simply as “the ‘threeway handshake’ that introduces distant and different computers to each other in a virtual space.” Later on, Cerf added an “Internet Protocol” to TCP which is used nowadays as TCP/IP.
